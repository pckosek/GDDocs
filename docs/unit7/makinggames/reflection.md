# Design Problem

When you began this handbook...

you probably wanted to learn how to make games.

Now you've reached the end.

You have learned about:

- programming
- systems
- design
- narrative
- worldbuilding
- iteration

But perhaps the most important question is no longer:

> **What can you build?**

Instead ask:

> **What kind of designer are you becoming?**

---

# Looking Back

Throughout this handbook we explored many different ideas.

We learned how to:

- represent information
- organize code
- build interactive systems
- generate worlds
- communicate through animation
- design levels
- create gameplay
- tell stories
- improve ideas through iteration

At first...

these topics may have seemed unrelated.

Now they form one connected way of thinking.

---

# What Changed?

Earlier in the handbook...

you may have thought:

```text
Programming

↓

Game
```

Now you might think:

```text
Ideas

↓

Systems

↓

Experiences

↓

Players
```

Programming became only one part of a much larger process.

Games are created by combining:

- engineering
- design
- art
- communication
- curiosity

---

# Design Principles

Along the way, several ideas appeared again and again.

```text
Represent Clearly.
```

Choose representations that make problems easier to solve.

---

```text
Separate Responsibilities.
```

Small systems working together are often easier to understand than one enormous system.

---

```text
Build Iteratively.
```

Games improve through observation and refinement.

---

```text
Teach Through Design.
```

Good games help players learn naturally.

---

```text
Create Meaningful Choices.
```

Interesting decisions create interesting gameplay.

---

```text
Design For People.
```

Every system ultimately exists to create an experience for another human being.

---

# A Different Way Of Thinking

Perhaps the biggest change is not technical.

It is the way you approach problems.

Instead of asking:

> **How do I build this?**

You might now ask:

> **Why does this exist?**

Instead of asking:

> **Which feature should I add?**

You might ask:

> **What experience am I trying to create?**

Instead of asking:

> **Is my code finished?**

You might ask:

> **What did this teach me?**

These questions often lead to better software...

and better games.

---

# Reflection

Think back to your first project.

How would you approach it differently today?

What ideas from this handbook would you apply?

Now think about your next project.

What do you want to explore?

- better systems?
- stronger narratives?
- procedural worlds?
- more thoughtful level design?
- better tools?
- entirely new genres?

Every finished project becomes the beginning of the next one.

---

# Looking Forward

Game development never truly ends.

Every completed game teaches something.

Every new project asks different questions.

Every player experiences your work differently.

That continual process of learning is one of the reasons many people continue creating games for their entire lives.

There will always be:

- another mechanic to explore
- another world to build
- another story to tell
- another problem worth solving

---

# Final Thought

Games are often described as collections of:

- mechanics
- graphics
- audio
- code

This handbook has argued something slightly different.

Games are collections of ideas.

Ideas become:

- representations

Representations become:

- systems

Systems create:

- experiences

Experiences become:

- memories

Long after players forget:

- variable names
- algorithms
- node hierarchies

they often remember:

- a discovery
- a challenge
- a place
- a feeling

Those moments are what game development ultimately creates.

---

# One Last Question

You now know how to build games.

You understand how games are designed.

You know how to improve them.

You know how to think about them.

The remaining question belongs only to you.

> **What kind of designer do you want to become?**
