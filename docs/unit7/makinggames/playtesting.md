# 7.5.4 Playtesting

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Making Games

**Difficulty:** Beginner

**Estimated Time:** 20–30 minutes

---

# Design Problem

Imagine you've finished your game.

You know how every mechanic works.

You know every puzzle.

You know where every secret is hidden.

Now another person plays it for the first time.

Within five minutes they become lost.

They misunderstand the controls.

They completely ignore the feature you were most excited about.

Who is correct?

The answer is surprising.

Both of you are experiencing different games.

The designer experiences the game through memory.

The player experiences it through discovery.

Playtesting allows us to see the game through the player's eyes.

---

# Why This Matters

Games are created for players.

Not for designers.

Playtesting reveals questions such as:

- Is the game understandable?
- Is it enjoyable?
- Is it too difficult?
- Is anything confusing?
- Are players behaving as expected?

Many of the most important design improvements are discovered through observation rather than planning.

---

# Mental Model

Think about writing directions to your house.

You know the route perfectly.

A visitor does not.

The only reliable way to discover confusing instructions is to watch someone follow them.

Games work exactly the same way.

```text
Designer

↓

Player

↓

Observation

↓

Improvement
```

The player teaches the designer.

---

# Example

Suppose your level contains:

- a hidden key
- a locked door
- a boss

You expect players to:

```text
Find Key

↓

Unlock Door

↓

Fight Boss
```

Instead they:

```text
Miss Key

↓

Become Lost

↓

Quit
```

The player has revealed something important.

The level may not communicate as clearly as you believed.

Playtesting uncovers these differences between intention and experience.

---

# Dissecting the Process

Notice the cycle.

```text
Build

↓

Observe

↓

Learn

↓

Improve

↓

Build Again
```

The goal is not to prove that the game works.

The goal is to discover how it can become better.

---

# Design Principle

## Observe players.

Don't explain the game.

During a playtest...

the designer's job is usually to watch.

If players become confused...

that confusion is valuable information.

Rather than immediately correcting the player...

ask yourself:

> **Why did the game allow this misunderstanding to happen?**

The game should communicate clearly enough that explanations become unnecessary.

---

# Practical Observation

Many beginning designers ask:

> "Did you like it?"

Experienced designers often ask:

- Where were you confused?
- What surprised you?
- What did you expect to happen?
- What did you try first?
- What did you completely ignore?

These questions reveal much more about the design.

Players often struggle in places the designer never anticipated.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Playtesting is bug testing."

Not quite.

Bug testing finds technical problems.

Playtesting explores the player's experience.

Both are important.

---

### "If players struggle, they're playing incorrectly."

Sometimes.

Other times...

the design may not be communicating clearly enough.

Confusion is valuable information.

---

### "The designer should explain the game."

Usually not during observation.

If every player requires explanation...

the design itself may need improvement.

---

### "One playtester is enough."

Different players notice different things.

Observing several people often reveals recurring patterns that deserve attention.

---

# Reflection

Imagine watching someone play your game.

What would you observe?

- Where do they hesitate?
- What do they misunderstand?
- What excites them?
- What do they ignore?

Now ask yourself:

Would you rather discover those problems today...

...or after releasing the game?
