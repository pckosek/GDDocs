# Design Problem

Imagine playing a game for three hours.

When you finish...

...nothing has changed.

You have:

- the same abilities
- the same equipment
- the same health
- the same challenges

Would you want to continue playing?

Many games answer this question through **progression**.

As players continue playing...

...the game changes with them.

---

# Why This Matters

Progression gives players a sense of growth.

Instead of repeating exactly the same experience...

...players gradually gain:

- new abilities
- better equipment
- increased knowledge
- access to new areas
- stronger characters

Progression transforms repeated play into a continuing journey.

---

# Mental Model

Think about learning a musical instrument.

Every practice session feels familiar.

Yet...

you slowly become more capable.

```text
Play

↓

Learn

↓

Improve

↓

New Possibilities

↓

Play Again
```

Games use progression in much the same way.

The player repeats familiar activities...

...while gradually becoming more capable.

---

# Common Forms of Progression

Games use many different forms of progression.

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Health | Increase survivability |
| Inventory | Carry more or better items |
| Abilities | Unlock new actions |
| Equipment | Improve effectiveness |
| Levels | Increase character statistics |
| World Access | Unlock new areas |
| Persistence | Save progress between sessions |

Different games emphasize different forms of progression.

Together they create the feeling that the player's journey is moving forward. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Progression strengthens the Core Loop.

# Design Principle

## Progression creates possibility.

Good progression does more than increase numbers.

It expands what the player can do.

New tools.

New areas.

New strategies.

The most satisfying progression often creates meaningful choices rather than simply making the player stronger.

---

# Practical Observation

Many beginning designers think progression means:

```text
+10 Health
```

Professional designers often think more broadly.

Examples include:

- unlocking a double jump
- gaining a grappling hook
- opening a new region
- learning a new spell
- discovering a crafting recipe

Progression changes the player's relationship with the game world.

Not just the player's statistics.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Progression always means leveling up."

No.

Many games have no experience levels at all.

Players may instead unlock:

- abilities
- equipment
- knowledge
- areas
- story

---

### "Progression only makes the player stronger."

Not necessarily.

Some progression introduces new challenges alongside new abilities.

Growth often creates new opportunities rather than simply reducing difficulty.

---

### "Saving the game is progression."

Saving preserves progression.

The progression itself comes from the changes made to the player's experience.

---

### "Every game needs progression."

Not always.

Some games focus on mastering a fixed set of mechanics.

Others rely heavily on long-term progression.

The appropriate choice depends on the experience the designer wants to create.
