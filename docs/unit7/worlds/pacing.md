# 7.2.5 Pacing

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Worlds

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

**Estimated Time:** 20–30 minutes

# Design Problem

Imagine a game that contains only combat.

Fight.

Fight.

Fight.

Fight.

Eventually...

even exciting combat begins to feel repetitive.

Now imagine a different sequence.

```text
Combat

↓

Quiet Exploration

↓

Puzzle

↓

Combat

↓

Story

↓

Boss
```

The mechanics may be similar...

...but the experience feels completely different.

This variation is called **Pacing**.

---

# Why This Matters

Pacing controls the rhythm of a game.

Rather than maintaining the same emotional intensity throughout...

...designers deliberately vary the player's experience.

Examples include:

- action
- exploration
- puzzles
- dialogue
- rest
- discovery

Changing the rhythm helps maintain attention and prevents repetition from becoming monotonous.

---

# Mental Model

Think about listening to music.

Not every moment is loud.

Not every moment is quiet.

Music becomes interesting because it changes.

Games work the same way.

```text
Challenge

↓

Recovery

↓

Discovery

↓

Challenge
```

The variation gives meaning to each individual moment.

---

# Example

Imagine a Zelda dungeon.

```text
Entrance

↓

Puzzle

↓

Combat

↓

Safe Room

↓

Mini Boss

↓

Treasure

↓

Boss
```

The player experiences many different emotions during the same level.

The rhythm becomes part of the design.

---

# Dissecting the Experience

Notice that pacing changes **when** different mechanics appear.

The mechanics themselves may never change.

Instead...

their arrangement changes.

```text
Action

↓

Rest

↓

Action

↓

Reflection

↓

Action
```

The order shapes the player's emotional experience.

---

# Design Principle

## Contrast creates engagement.

Constant excitement eventually stops feeling exciting.

Constant quiet eventually becomes boring.

Players remain engaged because the experience continually changes.

Good pacing balances:

- tension
- relief
- curiosity
- accomplishment

The transitions between these moments are just as important as the moments themselves.

---

# Practical Observation

Many beginning designers unintentionally create one emotional speed.

For example:

```text
Fight

↓

Fight

↓

Fight

↓

Fight
```

or

```text
Puzzle

↓

Puzzle

↓

Puzzle

↓

Puzzle
```

Although the mechanics may be enjoyable...

the overall experience often becomes predictable.

Introducing variety creates stronger emotional contrast.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Pacing means slowing the game down."

Not necessarily.

Pacing means controlling variation.

Sometimes the game becomes faster.

Sometimes slower.

Both are important.

---

### "Every level should follow the same rhythm."

Different levels often create different emotional experiences.

Some emphasize exploration.

Others focus on action.

Good pacing supports the goals of the level.

---

### "Pacing only matters in story-driven games."

Not at all.

Even games with very little narrative carefully control pacing through gameplay and level structure.

---

### "More action creates a better game."

Action is most exciting when contrasted with quieter moments.

Without contrast...

players quickly adapt to the intensity.
