# 7.2.6 Layering

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Worlds

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

**Estimated Time:** 20–30 minutes

---

# Design Problem

Imagine beginning a new game.

Within the first minute the player encounters:

- double jumps
- enemies
- moving platforms
- wall climbing
- puzzles
- keys
- switches
- boss fights

The game may contain excellent mechanics...

...but the player becomes overwhelmed.

Now imagine something different.

The game begins with:

```text
Walk
```

Later...

```text
Jump
```

Later...

```text
Enemy
```

Then...

```text
Moving Platform
```

Each new idea builds upon the previous one.

This technique is called **Layering**.

---

# Why This Matters

Layering introduces complexity gradually.

Rather than teaching everything immediately...

...the game allows players to master one idea before introducing the next.

This creates:

- confidence
- understanding
- mastery

Players spend less time feeling confused...

...and more time feeling capable.

---

# Mental Model

Think about learning mathematics.

You do not begin with calculus.

You first learn:

```text
Numbers

↓

Addition

↓

Multiplication

↓

Algebra

↓

Calculus
```

Each new idea depends upon the ones before it.

Games teach in exactly the same way.

---

# Example

Imagine introducing jumping.

```text
Flat Ground

↓

Small Gap

↓

Large Gap

↓

Moving Platform

↓

Enemy On Platform
```

The player learns:

- jumping

before

- precision

before

- timing

before

- combat while jumping.

Each challenge builds naturally upon the previous one.

---

# Dissecting the Design

Notice what changes.

The mechanic itself remains the same.

```text
Jump
```

What changes is the context.

```text
Jump

↓

Jump + Timing

↓

Jump + Enemy

↓

Jump + Puzzle

↓

Jump + Everything Together
```

The designer is not teaching new mechanics every moment.

They are gradually combining familiar ones.

---

# Design Principle

## Teach one new idea at a time.

Players learn most effectively when new concepts build upon existing knowledge.

Introducing one mechanic...

then combining it with earlier mechanics...

creates satisfying growth without unnecessary confusion.

Complexity emerges gradually.

---

# Practical Observation

Many beginning designers create difficult levels.

Experienced designers often create educational levels.

Every challenge quietly teaches something.

By the end of the level...

the player understands a mechanic they didn't fully appreciate at the beginning.

Good level design often feels like good teaching.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Layering means making the game easier."

Not at all.

Layering controls *when* complexity appears.

Later levels may become extremely challenging.

Players simply reach those challenges with the necessary understanding.

---

### "Every level should introduce a new mechanic."

No.

Many excellent levels deepen existing mechanics rather than introducing new ones.

Exploration of familiar ideas is often more satisfying than constant novelty.

---

### "Tutorials teach mechanics."

Tutorials are one form of teaching.

Entire games often continue teaching through thoughtful level design long after the tutorial has ended.

---

### "Players enjoy discovering everything themselves."

Sometimes.

But overwhelming players with too many simultaneous ideas often reduces curiosity rather than encouraging it.

Good teaching creates confidence.

---

# Reflection

Think about your favourite platformer.

How did it introduce:

- jumping?
- enemies?
- moving platforms?
- hazards?

Did the game present them all immediately?

Or did it gradually combine them into increasingly interesting challenges?

Now ask yourself:

How might you teach one new mechanic without using words?
