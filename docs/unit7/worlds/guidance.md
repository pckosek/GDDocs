# 7.2.4 Guidance

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Worlds

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

**Estimated Time:** 25–35 minutes

---

# Design Problem

Imagine entering a large cave.

There are no arrows.

No objective marker.

No minimap.

How does the player know where to go?

One solution would be to place a giant sign.

```text
↓

GO THIS WAY
```

It works...

...but it often breaks immersion.

Good level design usually communicates more subtly.

Instead of telling the player...

...the world quietly encourages them.

This is called **Guidance**.

---

# Why This Matters

Guidance helps players navigate naturally.

Rather than forcing exploration...

...it gently suggests where attention should go.

Good guidance allows players to feel:

- curious
- clever
- observant

The player believes they discovered the path themselves.

The designer quietly helped them all along.

---

# Mental Model

Think about entering a museum.

Nobody walks beside you saying:

> "Look over there."

Instead...

the building itself guides you.

Lighting.

Architecture.

Open spaces.

Interesting objects.

Games use exactly the same techniques.

```text
Environment

↓

Attention

↓

Decision
```

The level becomes another form of communication.

---

# Common Forms of Guidance

Designers use many subtle techniques.

| Technique | Suggests |
|-----------|----------|
| Lighting | Important locations |
| Coins or Collectibles | Safe paths or secrets |
| Enemies | Areas of importance |
| Architecture | Direction of travel |
| Landmarks | Long-term goals |
| Color | Interactive objects |
| Sound | Hidden events or objectives |

Each technique gently influences the player's decisions without removing their freedom.

---

# Dissecting the Design

Imagine a dark cave.

One tunnel contains:

- warm light
- footprints
- a few coins

The other contains:

- darkness
- broken rocks
- nothing interesting

Most players naturally investigate the first tunnel.

The designer never said:

> "Go this way."

The environment communicated that idea instead.

---

# Design Principle

## Suggest.

Don't command.

Good guidance rarely feels like instruction.

Instead...

the environment quietly rewards curiosity while helping players make informed decisions.

The player should feel that they discovered the path themselves.

---

# Practical Observation

Beginning designers often solve navigation problems with interface elements.

Examples include:

- arrows
- objective markers
- flashing icons

Sometimes these are appropriate.

However...

environmental guidance often creates a stronger sense of exploration because the world itself becomes readable.

Players begin trusting the environment rather than the interface.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Guidance means removing exploration."

Not at all.

Good guidance helps players make decisions.

It does not make those decisions for them.

---

### "The shortest path should always be obvious."

Sometimes.

Other times the designer intentionally encourages exploration before revealing the intended route.

The amount of guidance depends on the desired experience.

---

### "Only lighting guides players."

Many different systems contribute.

Examples include:

- architecture
- collectibles
- enemies
- sound
- animation
- landmarks

Good guidance often combines several subtle cues.

---

### "Players always notice guidance."

Usually they do not.

The most successful guidance often becomes invisible.

Players simply feel that the world makes sense.
