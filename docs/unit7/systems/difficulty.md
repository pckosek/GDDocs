# 7.3.5 Difficulty

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Systems

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

**Estimated Time:** 20–30 minutes

---

# Design Problem

Imagine two players.

The first has never played a platformer before.

The second has completed dozens.

They both begin the same level.

Will they experience the same difficulty?

Probably not.

The level has not changed.

The players have.

Difficulty is not simply a property of the game.

It emerges from the relationship between the player and the challenges they face.

---

# Why This Matters

Difficulty shapes the player's emotional experience.

If a game is:

- too easy

players may become bored.

If it is:

- too difficult

players may become frustrated.

Good designers continually balance challenge against the player's growing understanding and skill.

---

# Mental Model

Think about lifting weights.

A weight that challenges one person might be effortless for another.

The object has not changed.

Only the person lifting it has.

Games work in exactly the same way.

```text
Player Skill

↓

Challenge

↓

Difficulty
```

Difficulty exists where those two meet.

---

# Examples

Consider a simple platforming challenge.

```text
Small Gap
```

For a beginner...

this may feel difficult.

For an experienced player...

it may require almost no thought.

Now imagine adding:

- moving platforms
- enemies
- narrow ledges

The challenge increases.

Different players experience that increase differently.

---

# Dissecting the System

Notice the relationship.

```text
Player Skill

+

Game Challenge

↓

Difficulty
```

Neither component alone determines difficulty.

Both contribute.

Good game design continually adjusts one in response to the other.

---

# Design Principle

## Challenge should grow with the player.

As players become more skilled...

the game should gradually introduce:

- new mechanics
- harder combinations
- greater decision making
- more demanding situations

Difficulty becomes meaningful when it reflects the player's growing mastery rather than simply increasing numbers.

---

# Practical Observation

Many beginning designers increase difficulty by:

- giving enemies more health
- increasing damage
- adding more enemies

Sometimes these changes work.

Often...

more interesting difficulty comes from combining familiar mechanics in new ways.

For example:

```text
Jump

↓

Enemy

↓

Moving Platform

↓

Jump + Enemy + Moving Platform
```

The player applies existing knowledge in increasingly demanding situations.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Difficulty means making the game harder."

Not necessarily.

Difficulty is about providing an appropriate level of challenge.

Sometimes reducing difficulty creates a more enjoyable experience.

---

### "All players experience the same difficulty."

No.

Difficulty depends on the player's:

- experience
- knowledge
- skill
- familiarity with the mechanics

---

### "Health and damage determine difficulty."

They contribute.

Many other factors influence difficulty, including:

- timing
- information
- decision making
- level design
- pacing

---

### "Good games are very difficult."

Some are.

Others intentionally create relaxing or accessible experiences.

The appropriate difficulty depends on the goals of the game.

---

# Reflection

Think about a game you found difficult.

Why was it difficult?

Was it because of:

- fast reactions?
- complicated decisions?
- unfamiliar mechanics?
- limited resources?

Now imagine teaching that game to a beginner.

How might you gradually prepare them for its most challenging moments?
