# 7.3.2 Dynamics

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Systems

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

# Design Problem

Suppose your game contains one simple mechanic.

The player can:

```text
Jump
```

That mechanic tells us what the player is allowed to do.

But it tells us almost nothing about what the player will actually experience.

Will players:

- carefully time every jump?
- race through the level?
- experiment?
- take risks?

Those experiences are not mechanics.

They are **Dynamics**.

---

# Why This Matters

Dynamics describe how mechanics behave once players begin interacting with them.

Mechanics define the rules.

Dynamics emerge from those rules.

Examples include:

- cautious exploration
- aggressive combat
- resource conservation
- teamwork
- risk-taking
- speedrunning

Players create these experiences through play.

Designers create the conditions that allow them to emerge.

---

# Mental Model

Think about the rules of chess.

The rules never change.

Yet every game feels different.

```text
Rules

↓

Players

↓

Game
```

The rules are the mechanics.

The actual match is the dynamic.

The experience emerges from interaction.

---

# Example

Imagine a simple platformer.

Mechanics:

```text
Jump

Enemy

Coin
```

Now imagine different players.

One player:

```text
Avoids Every Enemy
```

Another:

```text
Defeats Every Enemy
```

Another:

```text
Collects Every Coin
```

The mechanics remain identical.

The dynamics become completely different.

---

# Dissecting the System

Notice the progression.

```text
Mechanics

↓

Player Decisions

↓

Dynamics

↓

Experience
```

Mechanics create possibilities.

Players explore those possibilities.

The resulting patterns of play become the game's dynamics.

---

# Design Principle

## Design rules.

Observe behaviour.

Game designers cannot completely control what players will do.

Instead...

they create systems that encourage certain behaviours.

Good designers continually observe players and adjust mechanics until the desired dynamics begin to appear naturally.

---

# Practical Observation

Many surprising moments in games were never explicitly designed.

Examples include:

- unexpected strategies
- creative problem solving
- cooperative teamwork
- speedrunning techniques
- unusual player behaviours

These dynamics emerge because players interact with systems in ways designers did not always predict.

Good systems leave room for discovery.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Dynamics are mechanics."

No.

Mechanics define the rules.

Dynamics describe what happens when players begin using those rules.

---

### "Designers control player behaviour."

Only partially.

Players continually surprise designers.

Dynamics emerge through interaction between players and mechanics.

---

### "Unexpected behaviour means the design failed."

Not necessarily.

Some of the most memorable gameplay emerges from creative uses of existing mechanics.

Unexpected dynamics often become beloved features.

---

### "Dynamics only appear in multiplayer games."

Not at all.

Single-player games also develop dynamics as players explore different strategies and play styles.

- move

What different play styles might emerge?

Would every player approach the game the same way?

- enemies

# Looking Back

Earlier we explored mechanics.

Mechanics describe what players can do.

Now we've discovered something more.

Players transform mechanics into experiences through their decisions.

Those patterns of behaviour are called dynamics.

Designing games is often less about writing rules...

...and more about understanding what those rules encourage players to do.
