# 7.3.4 Balance

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Systems

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

# Design Problem

Imagine creating a game with two weapons.

The first weapon deals:

```text
10 Damage
```

The second weapon deals:

```text
100 Damage
```

Both attack at the same speed.

Both have unlimited ammunition.

Which weapon will players choose?

The answer is obvious.

The first weapon has no meaningful purpose.

The game contains two weapons...

...but only one meaningful decision.

Good balance creates interesting choices.

---

# Why This Matters

Balance shapes the decisions players make.

Rather than ensuring everything is identical...

...good balance encourages players to consider:

- risk
- reward
- strategy
- timing
- opportunity

Different choices become valuable under different circumstances.

The game becomes richer because players must think rather than simply selecting the strongest option.

---

# Mental Model

Think about a toolbox.

A hammer is not "better" than a screwdriver.

Each solves a different problem.

Games work similarly.

```text
Situation

↓

Player Decision

↓

Appropriate Tool
```

Good balance gives every tool a meaningful purpose.

---

# Examples

Consider these weapons.

| Weapon | Strength | Weakness |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Sword | Fast attacks | Short range |
| Bow | Long range | Slower attacks |
| Hammer | High damage | Slow movement |

None is universally superior.

Each becomes valuable in different situations.

The interesting decision belongs to the player.

---

# Dissecting the System

Notice the relationship.

```text
Mechanics

↓

Player Choices

↓

Different Outcomes
```

If one option is always better...

the decision disappears.

Balance preserves meaningful choice.

---

# Design Principle

## Every choice should have a purpose.

Players should rarely ask:

> "Which option is strongest?"

Instead they should ask:

> **"Which option is best for this situation?"**

That question creates interesting gameplay.

---

# Practical Observation

Beginning designers often try to make every option equally powerful.

Experienced designers often do something different.

They create **trade-offs**.

Examples include:

- speed versus power
- safety versus reward
- range versus accuracy
- cost versus effectiveness

Trade-offs create decisions.

Decisions create gameplay.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Balanced means everything is equal."

No.

Balanced means different options remain useful in different situations.

---

### "Powerful options are bad."

Not necessarily.

Powerful options often require:

- greater skill
- limited resources
- higher risk
- longer recovery

Strength usually comes with meaningful trade-offs.

---

### "Balance is only for multiplayer games."

Single-player games also rely heavily on balance.

Players continually choose between:

- abilities
- equipment
- upgrades
- exploration
- combat strategies

Balance influences all of those decisions.

---

### "Balance can be solved mathematically."

Numbers help.

But balance is ultimately experienced by players.

Playtesting is often the most valuable balancing tool.
