# 7.3.3 Emergence

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Systems

# Design Problem

Imagine creating a game with only a few simple mechanics.

The player can:

- move
- jump
- collect items

Nothing seems particularly complicated.

Now players begin playing.

Some discover shortcuts.

Others invent challenges.

Some race through levels.

Others search every corner for secrets.

Where did these new behaviours come from?

The rules never changed.

The players changed.

This phenomenon is called **Emergence**.

---

# Why This Matters

Emergence occurs when simple systems produce surprisingly rich behaviour.

Rather than scripting every possible situation...

...the designer creates a small collection of mechanics.

Players combine those mechanics in creative and often unexpected ways.

Many memorable gameplay experiences emerge naturally from these interactions.

---

# Mental Model

Think about building with LEGO® bricks.

Each brick is simple.

Yet those simple pieces can become:

- houses
- castles
- spaceships
- robots

The complexity does not exist inside any individual brick.

It emerges from their combinations.

Games work the same way.

```text
Simple Mechanics

↓

Interaction

↓

Unexpected Possibilities
```

---

# Examples

Consider Minecraft.

The mechanics include:

- mining
- placing blocks
- crafting

These mechanics never explicitly say:

```text
Build A Castle
```

or

```text
Create A Computer
```

Players discover those possibilities themselves.

---

Consider Chess.

The rules are remarkably simple.

Yet people continue discovering new strategies centuries later.

The complexity emerges from interaction.

Not from additional rules.

# Dissecting the System

Notice the progression.

```text
Mechanics

↓

Dynamics

↓

Emergence

↓

Player Stories
```

The designer creates the mechanics.

Players create the stories.

Emergence bridges those two worlds.

---

# Design Principle

## Design possibilities, not outcomes.

Good designers rarely attempt to script every interesting moment.

Instead...

they create systems that encourage players to discover interesting possibilities for themselves.

The game becomes more surprising because not every experience was planned in advance.

---

# Practical Observation

Many memorable moments in games were never explicitly designed.

Players often discover:

- creative strategies
- unexpected shortcuts
- unusual combinations
- cooperative solutions
- playful experimentation

These moments emerge naturally because the mechanics interact in interesting ways.

Designers often embrace these discoveries rather than removing them.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Emergence means randomness."

No.

Emergence comes from interaction.

The rules may be completely deterministic.

Complex behaviour still appears.

---

### "Emergence cannot be designed."

Designers cannot directly create emergence.

They create systems that make emergence possible.

The interactions produce the complexity.

---

### "More mechanics create more emergence."

Not necessarily.

Many highly emergent games rely on surprisingly few mechanics.

The relationships between those mechanics matter far more than their number.

---

### "Emergence replaces game design."

No.

Emergence is one design goal among many.

Some games carefully script experiences.

Others encourage players to invent their own.

Both approaches are valuable.

# Looking Back

Earlier we explored mechanics and dynamics.

Mechanics define the rules.

Dynamics describe the behaviour that players create.

Emergence goes one step further.

Simple systems begin producing experiences that no single mechanic explicitly describes.

Some of the most memorable moments in games are not carefully scripted.

They are discovered.
