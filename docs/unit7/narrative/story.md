# 7.4.1 Story vs Narrative

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Narrative

**Difficulty:** Beginner

**Estimated Time:** 20–30 minutes

---

# Design Problem

Imagine telling a friend about your favourite game.

You might describe:

- what happened
- who you met
- how the adventure ended

Now imagine someone else telling exactly the same events...

...but in a completely different order.

Has the story changed?

Or has only the telling changed?

This distinction is one of the most important ideas in narrative design.

---

# Why This Matters

Games do more than contain stories.

They present those stories.

Understanding the difference between **story** and **narrative** helps designers think more carefully about how players experience events.

A game designer is not only deciding:

> **What happens?**

They are also deciding:

> **When does the player discover it?**

---

# Working Definitions

## Story

The **story** is the sequence of events that occurs in the game world.

It answers questions like:

- What happened?
- In what order?
- Why did those events occur?

The story exists whether anyone tells it or not.

---

## Narrative

The **narrative** is how the player experiences those events.

It answers questions like:

- What does the player learn first?
- What remains hidden?
- What is revealed later?

Narrative is the presentation of the story.

---

# Mental Model

Imagine a detective novel.

The events happened long before the detective arrives.

The story already exists.

The reader discovers those events gradually.

```text
Story

↓

Events

↓

Narrative

↓

Player Discovery
```

Games work in exactly the same way.

---

# Example

Imagine this simple story.

```text
The king dies.

↓

The kingdom falls.

↓

The hero restores peace.
```

That is the story.

Now consider different narratives.

Narrative A:

```text
The player witnesses the king's death.

↓

The kingdom collapses.

↓

The journey begins.
```

Narrative B:

```text
The player begins in the ruined kingdom.

↓

Discovers what happened.

↓

Learns about the king much later.
```

The story is identical.

The player's experience is completely different.

---

# Dissecting the Idea

Notice the distinction.

```text
Story

↓

Chronology
```

```text
Narrative

↓

Presentation
```

Designers often spend just as much time shaping **when** information is revealed as they do deciding **what** happens.

The order of discovery strongly influences emotion, mystery, and surprise.

---

# Design Principle

## Design the player's discovery.

Players rarely experience events in perfect chronological order.

Instead...

they gradually assemble the story through exploration, dialogue, gameplay, and observation.

Narrative is the design of that discovery process.

---

# Practical Observation

Not every game tells its story through dialogue.

Games may communicate through:

- exploration
- level design
- environmental details
- objects
- gameplay systems
- player actions

Even games with very little text often create memorable narratives because players construct meaning from their experiences.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Story and narrative are the same."

Not quite.

Story describes what happened.

Narrative describes how those events are presented to the player.

---

### "Games need cutscenes to tell stories."

Not at all.

Many games communicate through:

- environments
- mechanics
- exploration
- player choice

Narrative extends far beyond dialogue.

---

### "Games only tell one story."

Many games contain several.

There may be:

- the designer's story
- the world's history
- the player's personal journey

These narratives often overlap.

---

### "Narrative only matters in story-driven games."

Every game creates narratives.

Even games with no written story produce memorable sequences of events that players later recount to others.

Players naturally construct stories from play.

Think about a memorable moment from a game.

Did it happen because:

- of dialogue?
- a cutscene?
- your own decisions?
- something unexpected?

Now ask yourself:

Were you remembering the **story**...

...or the way you experienced discovering it?
