# Design Problem

Imagine trying to write a story.

Where should it begin?

When should conflict appear?

How should it end?

There is no single correct answer.

Over thousands of years...

writers, filmmakers, and designers have noticed that many successful stories share similar patterns.

These patterns are called **Narrative Structures**.

They help us understand how stories are organized.

They are not rules that every story must follow.

---

# Why This Matters

Narrative structures provide a vocabulary for discussing stories.

They help designers think about questions such as:

- Where does the conflict begin?
- When should information be revealed?
- How does the character change?
- How should the experience end?

These structures describe common patterns.

They do not prescribe a single way to tell stories.

---

# Mental Model

Think about architecture.

Many buildings contain:

- foundations
- walls
- roofs

Yet no two buildings are identical.

Narrative structures work similarly.

```text
Structure

↓

Supports Story

↓

Does Not Replace Creativity
```

The framework provides support.

The story gives it life.

# Common Narrative Structures

Several frameworks appear frequently throughout literature, film, and games.

### Three Act Structure

```text
Setup

↓

Confrontation

↓

Resolution
```

A simple model describing:

- introduction
- conflict
- conclusion

---

### Todorov's Narrative Model

```text
Equilibrium

↓

Disruption

↓

Recognition

↓

Attempt To Repair

↓

New Equilibrium
```

This structure emphasizes how stories move from stability through conflict toward a changed world. 

---

### Harmon's Story Circle

```text
Comfort

↓

Need

↓

Go

↓

Search

↓

Find

↓

Take

↓

Return

↓

Change
```

This model focuses on the transformation of the main character throughout the journey. 

---

### Hero's Journey

Many myths and adventures share a broader pattern in which an ordinary individual leaves the familiar world, faces trials, grows through adversity, and returns transformed.

Although widely influential...

it is only one of many ways stories can be understood. 

---

# Dissecting the Idea

Notice that these structures describe different aspects of storytelling.

Some emphasize:

```text
Conflict
```

Others emphasize:

```text
Character Change
```

Others focus on:

```text
Player Discovery
```

No single framework completely explains every story.

Each highlights different relationships within the narrative.

---

# Design Principle

## Use structures to understand stories.

Not to constrain them.

Narrative structures provide useful ways of thinking about storytelling.

Good designers often study several frameworks...

...then choose the ideas that best support the experience they wish to create.

The framework serves the story.

Never the other way around.

---

# Practical Observation

Many successful games borrow ideas from several narrative structures simultaneously.

For example:

- a Three Act overall progression
- a Hero's Journey for the protagonist
- smaller Story Circles within individual quests

These frameworks often overlap rather than compete.

Designers use them as analytical tools rather than strict templates.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Every story follows one structure."

Not necessarily.

Many stories blend several structures or intentionally avoid traditional patterns altogether.

---

### "Narrative structures are formulas."

No.

They describe common patterns observed across many stories.

They are tools for understanding rather than recipes for creativity.

---

### "Games must follow film structures."

Games introduce interaction, exploration, and player agency.

These often reshape traditional narrative structures in interesting ways.

---

### "Breaking the structure is always better."

Not necessarily.

Sometimes familiar structures help players understand the story.

Other times experimentation creates entirely new experiences.

The appropriate choice depends on the goals of the designer.
