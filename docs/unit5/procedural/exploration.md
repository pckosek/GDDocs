# Design Problem

Throughout this unit we've explored many computational strategies.

We learned about:

- randomness
- local rules
- traversal
- sampling
- fields

It is tempting to think of these as individual algorithms.

They are not.

They are **ways of thinking**.

The real question is no longer:

> **Which algorithm should I use?**

Instead, it becomes:

> **What process do I want to create?**

---

# Why This Matters

Many beginning programmers search for existing algorithms.

Professional programmers often do something different.

They combine ideas.

For example:

```text
Randomness

+

Traversal

↓

Maze
```

Or

```text
Sampling

+

Fields

↓

Forest
```

Or

```text
Local Rules

+

Randomness

↓

Cave
```

Interesting procedural systems rarely come from one algorithm.

They emerge from combining computational ideas in new ways.

# Design Principle

## Design processes rather than artifacts.

Rather than asking:

> "How do I generate a cave?"

ask:

> **"What simple process might naturally produce cave-like structures?"**

Rather than asking:

> "How do I generate a forest?"

ask:

> **"What relationships should trees have with one another?"**

Programming becomes much more interesting when we stop designing finished products...

...and begin designing the systems that create them.

# Practical Observation

Many well-known procedural algorithms were originally experiments.

Someone asked:

> "What happens if..."

- every cell follows this rule?
- points repel one another?
- terrain grows this way?
- samples move over time?
- several fields combine?

Those questions led to entirely new computational techniques.

Exploration is not separate from programming.

Exploration is often how programming advances.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Procedural generation is a collection of algorithms."

Not really.

Algorithms are examples.

The deeper ideas are the computational strategies they use.

---

### "There is one correct way to generate a world."

No.

There are countless ways.

Different computational processes produce different artifacts.

Part of the creative process is discovering new ones.

---

### "Good procedural systems require advanced mathematics."

Not necessarily.

Many beautiful procedural systems emerge from remarkably simple rules combined thoughtfully.

Complexity often emerges naturally.

---

### "The goal is reproducing existing algorithms."

Learning existing algorithms is valuable.

But understanding **why** they work makes it possible to invent entirely new ones.

That is where procedural generation becomes creative.
