**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

# Design Problem

Imagine two games.

One contains:

- dragons
- ancient ruins
- magical swords

The other contains:

- spaceships
- artificial intelligence
- distant planets

They seem completely different.

Yet somehow...

...both feel strangely familiar.

Why?

Because beneath the setting...

they often tell the same kinds of stories.

These recurring patterns are called **Myths**.

---

# Why This Matters

Myths are more than old stories.

They are recurring patterns that explore fundamental human experiences.

Examples include:

- growing up
- sacrifice
- discovery
- temptation
- courage
- loss
- hope

These ideas appear repeatedly across cultures, genres, and historical periods.

Games continue drawing upon these patterns because they remain meaningful to players.

---

# Mental Model

Think about music.

The same melody may be played by:

- a piano
- a violin
- an orchestra

The instruments change.

The melody remains recognizable.

Myths work similarly.

```text
Ancient Pattern

↓

Modern Story

↓

Player Experience
```

The setting changes.

The underlying pattern often remains.

---

# Examples

Many games explore familiar mythic ideas.

| Theme | Possible Game Example |
|---------|-----------------------|
| Journey Into The Unknown | Exploration games |
| Hero Faces Impossible Odds | Adventure games |
| Fall And Redemption | Role-playing games |
| Discovery Of Hidden Knowledge | Puzzle games |
| Sacrifice For Others | Narrative adventures |

Notice that none of these requires:

- castles
- magic
- ancient civilizations

Mythic patterns appear in science fiction, historical fiction, fantasy, and even modern settings.

---

# Dissecting the Idea

Notice that myths are not simply stories.

They are patterns.

```text
Challenge

↓

Transformation

↓

Return
```

or

```text
Curiosity

↓

Discovery

↓

Responsibility
```

Many different stories can express exactly the same underlying structure.

Players recognize these patterns even when they never consciously think about them.

---

# Design Principle

## Familiar patterns help players understand new worlds.

Completely unfamiliar worlds can feel confusing.

Mythic structures provide something recognizable beneath the surface.

They allow players to understand:

- characters
- conflicts
- journeys

without requiring lengthy explanations.

Familiar patterns create emotional resonance.

---

# Practical Observation

Many successful games borrow mythic ideas without directly adapting ancient myths.

Designers often revisit themes such as:

- growth
- responsibility
- identity
- belonging
- sacrifice

These themes continue appearing because they describe experiences that remain meaningful across cultures and generations.

The myth survives even when the setting changes completely.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Myth means ancient mythology."

Not necessarily.

Ancient myths are one source.

Modern stories often create entirely new myths using familiar narrative patterns.

---

### "Every game needs a hero."

No.

Some games explore:

- communities
- civilizations
- families
- ordinary individuals

Mythic patterns extend far beyond traditional heroes.

---

### "Myths are unrealistic."

Many myths describe emotional truths rather than historical events.

Their power often comes from the ideas they explore rather than their factual accuracy.

---

### "Using myth makes stories unoriginal."

Not at all.

Every generation reinterprets familiar ideas in new settings with new values and new perspectives.

The pattern remains.

The expression changes.
