# 7.4.3 Character

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Narrative

**Difficulty:** Beginner

# Design Problem

Imagine watching a film.

The main character begins exactly the same person...

...and ends exactly the same person.

They learn nothing.

They overcome nothing.

Nothing about them changes.

Would their journey still feel meaningful?

Probably not.

Stories are often remembered because characters change.

The events matter.

But the transformation matters even more.

---

# Why This Matters

Characters give players someone to understand, support, or even challenge.

Whether the character is:

- a hero
- a villain
- a companion
- the player themselves

they help give meaning to the events of the story.

Many memorable characters are not defined by their strengths.

They are defined by how those strengths and weaknesses change over time.

---

# Mental Model

Think about climbing a mountain.

Reaching the summit is important.

But the climb changes the climber.

Stories work similarly.

```text
Character

↓

Experience

↓

Change
```

The events matter because they transform someone.

---

# Character Change

Many stories follow a simple pattern.

```text
Character

↓

Challenge

↓

Decision

↓

Change
```

The character encounters difficulty.

They make choices.

Those choices gradually transform who they become.

Without change...

the journey often loses much of its emotional impact.

---

# Examples

Consider these familiar character journeys.

```text
Fear

↓

Courage
```

---

```text
Selfishness

↓

Responsibility
```

---

```text
Inexperience

↓

Wisdom
```

Although the stories differ...

they all describe transformation.

The events are important because they shape the character.

---

# Dissecting the Idea

Notice that stories often contain two journeys.

```text
External Journey

↓

What Happens
```

and

```text
Internal Journey

↓

Who Changes
```

The external journey creates action.

The internal journey creates meaning.

Good stories often connect both.

---

# Design Principle

## Characters are remembered because they change.

Interesting characters are rarely perfect.

They:

- struggle
- fail
- learn
- adapt

The player's emotional connection often comes from witnessing that transformation.

Growth gives events significance.

---

# Practical Observation

Games often tell character stories differently than films.

Players may influence:

- choices
- relationships
- exploration
- moral decisions

This means the player's actions frequently become part of the character's development.

Games allow players to participate in transformation rather than simply observing it.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Characters must be heroes."

Not at all.

Stories may focus on:

- villains
- companions
- ordinary people
- entire communities

Any perspective can create meaningful narrative.

---

### "Interesting characters need complicated backstories."

Not necessarily.

Simple characters can become memorable through meaningful choices and believable growth.

---

### "Characters only change during cutscenes."

Games often communicate character development through:

- gameplay
- exploration
- player decisions
- environmental storytelling

Change occurs throughout the experience.

---

### "The player character and the player are the same."

Sometimes.

Other times they are very different.

Games continually explore the relationship between player identity and character identity.

---

# Reflection

Think about a memorable game character.

How were they different at the end of the story than they were at the beginning?

Now imagine that they never changed.

Would the story feel the same?

Why?

---

# Looking Back

Earlier we explored narrative structures.

Those structures organize events.

Now we've discovered something equally important.

Events matter because they affect people.

Characters give players someone whose growth, struggle, and transformation create emotional meaning.

The story changes the character.

The character changes the player's understanding of the story.
