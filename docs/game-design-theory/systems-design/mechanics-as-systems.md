# 7.3.1 Mechanics

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Systems

**Difficulty:** Beginner

**Estimated Time:** 20–30 minutes

---

# Design Problem

Suppose we create a game with one mechanic.

The player can:

```text
Jump
```

Is the game finished?

Probably not.

Now suppose we add:

- enemies
- coins
- moving platforms
- checkpoints

The player is still jumping.

But suddenly...

the experience feels completely different.

Why?

Because mechanics rarely exist alone.

They become meaningful through the systems they participate in.

---

# Why This Matters

Mechanics are the smallest units of player interaction.

Examples include:

- movement
- jumping
- collecting
- attacking
- building
- interacting

Every game is built from mechanics.

Understanding them is the first step toward understanding how games create experiences.

---

# Mental Model

Think about a single musical note.

One note has meaning.

Many notes create music.

Mechanics work similarly.

```text
Mechanic

↓

System

↓

Experience
```

The mechanic is important.

The system gives it purpose.

---

# Examples

Consider the mechanic:

```text
Jump
```

Now place it into different games.

### Platformer

```text
Jump

↓

Avoid Hazard
```

---

### Puzzle Game

```text
Jump

↓

Reach Switch
```

---

### Action Game

```text
Jump

↓

Avoid Attack
```

The mechanic remains identical.

The surrounding system changes.

The player experiences something entirely different.

---

# Dissecting the Idea

Mechanics answer:

> **What can the player do?**

They do **not** answer:

> **Why should the player do it?**

Nor do they answer:

> **What happens afterwards?**

Those questions belong to systems.

Mechanics provide actions.

Systems provide meaning.

---

# Design Principle

## Mechanics gain meaning through relationships.

A mechanic becomes interesting because of how it interacts with:

- other mechanics
- the environment
- progression
- rewards
- player goals

Designing mechanics in isolation rarely produces memorable games.

Designing relationships often does.

---

# Practical Observation

Beginning designers often brainstorm mechanics.

For example:

- climbing
- crafting
- dialogue
- swimming
- magic

Experienced designers usually ask another question.

> **What does this mechanic interact with?**

That question often reveals whether the mechanic truly belongs in the game.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "More mechanics create a better game."

Not necessarily.

Many memorable games rely on very few mechanics.

The relationships between those mechanics often matter far more.

---

### "Mechanics are gameplay."

Mechanics contribute to gameplay.

Gameplay emerges from the interaction of many mechanics working together.

---

### "Every mechanic should be unique."

Many games reuse familiar mechanics.

The originality often comes from how those mechanics interact rather than from inventing entirely new ones.

---

### "Mechanics should be designed independently."

Usually not.

Mechanics become much stronger when designed alongside the systems that support them.

---

# Reflection

Examples include:

- jumping

- attacking
- crafting
- dialogue

Now ask yourself:

What other mechanics make this one interesting?

Could the mechanic exist by itself?

Or does it depend on a larger system?
