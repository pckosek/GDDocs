# 7.2.3 Safe Spaces

## Classification

**Type:** Design / Engineering Guide

**Category:** Designing Worlds

**Difficulty:** Beginner

**Estimated Time:** 20–30 minutes

---

# Design Problem

Imagine fighting through a difficult dungeon.

The player survives several enemies.

Solves a puzzle.

Avoids traps.

Should the next room immediately contain another battle?

Perhaps not.

Sometimes the player needs time to:

- recover
- think
- explore
- prepare

These moments are called **Safe Spaces**.

They temporarily remove immediate danger while allowing the player to regroup.

---

# Why This Matters

Games are built from changing rhythms.

Moments of action become more exciting when followed by moments of calm.

Safe Spaces allow players to:

- recover health
- manage inventory
- save progress
- explore the environment
- absorb the story
- prepare for the next challenge

Without moments of rest...

continuous challenge often becomes exhausting rather than exciting.

---

# Mental Model

Think about climbing a mountain.

You do not sprint continuously to the summit.

Occasionally...

you stop.

You catch your breath.

You look around.

Then you continue climbing.

Games work in much the same way.

```text
Challenge

↓

Safe Space

↓

Prepare

↓

Challenge
```

The quiet moments give meaning to the difficult ones.

---

# Examples

Safe Spaces appear in many different forms.

| Example | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Save Room | Preserve progress |
| Friendly Village | Rest and prepare |
| Campfire | Recover resources |
| Puzzle Room | Change the pace |
| Scenic Vista | Encourage reflection |
| Shop | Upgrade equipment |

Although these locations appear different...

...they all temporarily reduce pressure.

---

# Dissecting the Design

Notice what changes.

The player still explores.

The game continues.

Only one thing disappears.

```text
Immediate Threat
```

Removing danger changes the player's priorities.

Instead of asking:

> **How do I survive?**

the player begins asking:

> **What should I do next?**

Safe Spaces create opportunities for planning rather than reacting.

---

# Design Principle

## Rest makes challenge meaningful.

Players appreciate difficult moments because they have opportunities to recover between them.

A well-designed game balances:

- tension
- relief

Neither should exist continuously.

Contrast creates pacing.

---

# Practical Observation

Many beginning designers place enemies everywhere.

The result is often:

```text
Fight

↓

Fight

↓

Fight

↓

Fight
```

Although challenging...

the experience becomes repetitive.

Introducing Safe Spaces naturally creates:

```text
Challenge

↓

Recovery

↓

Challenge

↓

Discovery
```

The level begins to breathe.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Safe Spaces are boring."

Not necessarily.

They often contain some of the most memorable moments in a game because the player finally has time to observe the world.

---

### "Nothing should happen in a Safe Space."

Many important activities occur there.

Examples include:

- conversations
- upgrades
- exploration
- saving
- storytelling

Only immediate danger is removed.

---

### "Safe Spaces reduce difficulty."

Not directly.

They change the pacing.

The difficult sections remain challenging.

Players simply have opportunities to prepare between them.

---

### "Every game needs frequent Safe Spaces."

Different games create different rhythms.

Horror games often use them sparingly.

Exploration games may use them frequently.

The appropriate balance depends on the intended experience.

---

# Reflection

Think about one of your favourite games.

Where does the game allow the player to relax?

Why was that moment placed there?

Now imagine removing every Safe Space.

Would the game become:

- more exciting?
- more exhausting?
- less memorable?

Why?
