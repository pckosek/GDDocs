# Design Problem

Imagine spending three years creating a game.

Eventually...

you release it.

Now another question appears.

How will the project continue?

Will players:

- buy the game once?
- subscribe?
- purchase expansions?
- support it through cosmetic items?
- play it for free?

Creating a game is only one part of game development.

Developers must also decide how the project will be sustained.

This decision is called the **Business Model**.

---

# Why This Matters

Business models influence how games are developed, supported, and updated.

They affect questions such as:

- How does the studio earn revenue?
- How long will the game receive updates?
- What content will be added later?
- How will players access the game?

Understanding these models helps designers better understand the wider game industry.

---

# Common Business Models

Several business models appear frequently in modern game development.

| Model | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Premium | One-time purchase for the complete game |
| Subscription | Recurring payment for continued access |
| Free-to-Play | Free to begin playing, with other revenue sources |
| Expansion / DLC | Additional content purchased after release |
| Ad-Supported | Revenue generated through advertising |

Each model creates different opportunities and different challenges for both players and developers. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

# Monetization Strategies

A business model answers:

> **How do players access the game?**

A monetization strategy answers:

> **How does the game continue generating revenue?**

Examples include:

- cosmetic purchases
- expansion content
- virtual currency
- live service updates
- optional purchases

These strategies influence how games continue evolving after release. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

# Mental Model

Think about a library.

Books may be:

- purchased
- borrowed
- subscribed to

The stories remain stories.

Only the method of access changes.

Games work similarly.

```text
Game

↓

Business Model

↓

Player Access
```

The business model changes how players obtain and support the game.

It does not define the gameplay itself.

---

# Dissecting the Idea

Notice that different models encourage different development patterns.

For example:

```text
Premium

↓

Complete Experience
```

while

```text
Live Service

↓

Ongoing Updates
```

Neither approach is universally better.

Each supports different kinds of projects and player communities.

---

# Design Principle

## Choose a model that supports the experience you want to create.

Business decisions should support the long-term goals of the project.

Different games naturally suit different business models.

The important question is not:

> **Which model earns the most?**

Instead ask:

> **Which model best supports this game's design and its players?**

# Practical Observation

Most student projects never need to consider monetization.

That is perfectly appropriate.

However...

understanding business models helps explain why commercial games often receive:

- updates
- downloadable content
- seasonal events
- cosmetic additions
- expansions

These decisions are often connected to the way the project is sustained after release.

# Common Misconceptions

### "Every successful game is Free-to-Play."

No.

Successful games exist under many different business models.

The appropriate choice depends on the project and its goals.

---

### "Business models determine quality."

Not necessarily.

Excellent and disappointing games exist under every business model.

The model influences development.

It does not guarantee quality.

---

### "Monetization and business models are the same."

They are related.

A business model describes how players access the game.

Monetization describes how the project generates ongoing revenue.

---

### "Business is separate from design."

Often the two influence one another.

Design decisions and business decisions frequently interact throughout development.

Understanding both helps explain why different games evolve differently after release.
