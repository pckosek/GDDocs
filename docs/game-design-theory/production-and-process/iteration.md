# Design Problem

Imagine you have finished your game.

The controls work.

The graphics are complete.

The levels are playable.

Should you release it immediately?

Perhaps not.

After watching several people play...

you notice:

- players become lost
- jumps feel awkward
- enemies are too difficult
- one mechanic is unexpectedly enjoyable

The game has revealed something.

It is teaching you how it wants to be improved.

This process is called **Iteration**.

---

# Why This Matters

Very few games become excellent during the first implementation.

Instead...

they improve through repeated cycles of:

- building
- observing
- refining
- testing

Each iteration teaches the designer something new.

Good games are rarely discovered all at once.

They gradually emerge through many thoughtful improvements.

---

# Mental Model

Think about sculpting.

A sculptor rarely begins with the finished statue.

Instead they gradually remove, reshape, and refine the material.

Games develop in much the same way.

```text
Build

↓

Observe

↓

Improve

↓

Repeat
```

Each iteration reveals the next improvement.

---

# Example

Imagine building a platformer.

Iteration One:

```text
Movement Works
```

Iteration Two:

```text
Jump Feels Better
```

Iteration Three:

```text
Enemy Placement Improved
```

Iteration Four:

```text
Camera Smoother
```

The finished game is not the result of one perfect idea.

It is the result of many small improvements working together.

---

# Dissecting the Process

Notice the difference.

Beginning designers often imagine:

```text
Perfect Design

↓

Implementation
```

Professional designers often experience:

```text
Implementation

↓

Discovery

↓

Improvement
```

The design evolves as the game is built.

The game itself becomes part of the design process.

---

# Design Principle

## Design through discovery.

The first version of a mechanic is rarely the final version.

Every playtest provides evidence.

Every observation suggests improvements.

Iteration transforms assumptions into understanding.

The designer gradually discovers what the game wants to become.

---

# Practical Observation

Iteration rarely means rebuilding everything.

Often the most valuable improvements are surprisingly small.

Examples include:

- moving one platform
- adjusting jump height
- reducing enemy health
- improving feedback
- changing pacing
- clarifying guidance

Many small improvements often produce a much stronger game than one dramatic redesign.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Iteration means the original design failed."

Not at all.

Iteration is a normal part of professional game development.

Good designers expect their games to change.

---

### "The first idea is usually the best."

Sometimes.

More often...

the strongest ideas emerge after observing players interacting with the game.

---

### "Iteration never ends."

Eventually every project reaches a point where further improvements produce only small gains.

Good designers also learn when the game is ready to ship.

---

### "Changing the game means starting over."

Usually not.

Most iteration builds upon the existing design rather than replacing it completely.

Small adjustments often have surprisingly large effects.
