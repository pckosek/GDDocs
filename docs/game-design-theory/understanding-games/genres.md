# Design Problem

Suppose someone asks:

> "What kind of game are you making?"

You might answer:

- Platformer
- RPG
- Strategy
- Simulation

These labels are called **genres**.

Genres provide a shared vocabulary for describing games.

They help players understand what kinds of experiences they can expect before they even begin playing.

---

# Why This Matters

Genres communicate expectations.

When players hear:

```text
Platformer
```

they immediately expect:

- jumping
- movement
- obstacles
- precision

When they hear:

```text
Strategy
```

they expect something very different.

Genres help designers communicate ideas quickly while giving players familiar expectations.

---

# Common Genres

Some of the most common genres include:

| Genre | Primary Player Experience |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Platformer | Movement and precise traversal |
| Role-Playing Game (RPG) | Character progression and player choice |
| Strategy | Planning and long-term decision making |
| Simulation | Modeling real or imagined systems |
| Action-Adventure | Exploration combined with action |
| Shooter | Combat through aiming and precision |
| Puzzle | Logic and problem solving |
| Sports | Competition based on sporting activities |
| Casual | Accessible, short play sessions |
| Mobile | Designed primarily for phones and tablets |
| Serious Game | Learning, training, or social impact |
| MMO | Persistent online worlds shared by many players |

# Mental Model

Think about books.

When someone says:

```text
Mystery
```

you expect one kind of story.

When someone says:

```text
Science Fiction
```

you expect another.

Game genres work similarly.

```text
Genre

↓

Player Expectations

↓

Gameplay Experience
```

Genres provide a common language between designers and players.

---

# Dissecting the Idea

Notice that genres are not defined by graphics.

A platformer may be:

- 2D
- 3D
- pixel art
- hand drawn
- realistic

The artwork changes.

The core player experience remains recognizable.

Likewise, two RPGs may look completely different while still sharing many of the same design ideas.

---

# Design Principle

## Genres describe experiences, not rules.

Genres help us understand the primary focus of a game.

They are useful guides...

...not strict definitions.

Many modern games intentionally combine ideas from several genres to create new experiences.

---

# Practical Observation

Modern games rarely belong to only one genre.

For example:

| Game | Possible Genres |
|------|-----------------|
| Minecraft | Survival, Sandbox, Simulation |
| Stardew Valley | Simulation, RPG |
| Terraria | Platformer, Survival, Crafting |
| Zelda | Action-Adventure, Puzzle |
| Portal | Puzzle, First-Person |

Thinking in terms of combinations often produces more interesting game designs than trying to fit perfectly into a single category.

---

# Common Misconceptions

### "Every game belongs to one genre."

Not necessarily.

Many successful games blend ideas from several genres.

---

### "Genres limit creativity."

Genres provide expectations.

Designers are free to follow, combine, or deliberately challenge those expectations.

---

### "Graphics determine genre."

No.

Genres describe the player's experience.

Visual style is a separate design decision.

---

### "Genres never change."

Genres constantly evolve.

As designers experiment and combine ideas, entirely new genres and subgenres emerge.
